The trouble with the word "agent"

In a single week I've heard agent used for a chatbot that calls one tool, a long-running planner that books a flight, and — somewhat to my surprise — a Zapier with an LLM in the middle.

I keep getting stuck on the word agent. Not because it's wrong, exactly, but because it's doing too much. In a single week I've heard it used for: a chatbot that calls one tool, a long-running process that books a flight, an LLM with a system prompt, a multi-step planner with a verifier loop, and — somewhat to my surprise — a Zapier-style if-this-then-that with an LLM in the middle. These are not the same kind of thing. They don't have the same failure modes, they don't take the same kinds of input, and they don't reward the same kinds of design attention. This is a note about why that matters, and a small attempt to pull the word apart.

The first thing I notice is that the word has eaten three or four other words. Tool use is now agent. Workflow is now agent. Assistant is now agent. Bot is now agent, but only the ones we think are clever. The semantic compression is not because these things have converged — they haven't — but because the marketing convergence happened first and the design vocabulary is catching up.

"The word agent is doing the work of about six older words at once. That's not a vocabulary problem; that's a design problem in disguise."

Three things that aren't the same

Consider three systems I've worked on in the last year, all of which were called agents in their respective decks:

One was a chat interface that, when asked a question, called a single retrieval tool, formatted the result, and returned it. One round-trip. No memory. No state. The interesting design questions were about citation surface, source provenance, and the design of the "I don't know" failure mode. None of these are agentic questions in any deep sense; they are the same questions you'd ask of any retrieval-backed Q&A interface. Calling it an agent was branding.

Two was a long-running planner that took a user's stated goal — book a series of related appointments around a constraint — and worked through it over fifteen or twenty turns, sometimes asking for clarification, sometimes acting unilaterally, sometimes stalling. The interesting design questions here were about state visibility (what is it doing right now?), recoverability (can I rewind?), and negotiated authority (when does it ask, when does it just do?). These are deeply agentic questions. They have very little in common with the questions in case one.

Three was, structurally, a chain of conditionals with an LLM call at one node. Trigger, check, generate, write to a system of record, notify. The LLM at the node was doing classification, not reasoning. The "agency" was vestigial — the system looked agentic from outside, because it took input and produced action, but the LLM could have been a decision tree from 2018 and the system would have run the same. The design questions were workflow questions: error handling, monitoring, idempotency. Calling it an agent was, again, branding.

Why this matters for the design surface

Each of these systems failed differently when it failed, and each rewarded a different kind of design attention. The first failed by being confidently wrong; the design lever was provenance. The second failed by going quiet for ninety seconds with no indication of what it was doing; the design lever was state visibility. The third failed by triggering on the wrong row; the design lever was classification confidence.

If you walk in with the word agent as your design frame, you will reach for the same kit for all three: a chat surface, a "thinking…" indicator, a memory feature you don't need, a personality you also don't need. This is what bad design vocabulary does. It compresses the design space before you've looked at it.

The fix is not a better word. The fix is the discipline of asking which kind of agent before you ask what should this agent be like. The Anthropic team has been good about this — their distinction between workflows and agents is useful and worth borrowing. But even that is too coarse for what shows up in the wild. The honest answer most weeks is that the system in front of you is several things at once, glued together with English.

The design move I keep coming back to is to ignore the word and look at the trace. What does the system actually do, step by step, in the median case? In the bad case? When it doesn't know? When it's wrong? Those four traces — median, failure, uncertainty, error — tell you what kind of thing you're designing for, regardless of what the deck called it.

Jody has returned to evaluation as a design activity — not an engineering one — in three essays and a recurring note thread. The core claim, articulated most directly in "Reading a hundred traces" (May 2026), is that an eval rubric is fundamentally a statement about what counts as good behavior — and that statement is a design artifact, not a measurement artifact.

She's been most pointed about this in the context of LLM-as-judge setups: in a note from early May she wrote that "an LLM-as-judge eval is a user interview where the user is also a hallucinator." There's also a thread of work, mostly in the anonymized briefs, about the construction of golden sets and the politics of who gets to define a failure category. That work is less abstract; it's about who in the room has the authority to say "this output is wrong."

§ 01 — context

The work

A national U.S. pharmacy retailer was piloting a multi-turn voice and text agent for three intertwined tasks — refill requests, transfer initiation, and adherence check-ins. The model was already chosen; the integration with the patient-record system was already half-built. What was missing was a way to tell whether the thing was actually good enough to ship to a regulated audience.

I came in to design the evaluation harness — not as a test suite, but as the team's evolving definition of what good behavior meant. The brief was nominally engineering; the real work was design.

§ 02 — the constraint that mattered

"Working" and "good" were not the same thing

The team had been measuring task completion. Did the refill go through? Did the transfer succeed? Did the patient confirm? Those numbers were already at 92%. The pilot was, by that measure, working.

But when I sat with the recordings, the failure modes I cared about lived inside the 92%. The agent had completed the refill — and along the way, mis-pronounced the patient's name, missed a cue that the patient was confused, and offered a generic adherence reminder that contradicted what the doctor had told them last week. Task completion was a true statement and a useless one.

§ 03 — approach

An eval rubric, designed before it was instrumented

I treated the eval rubric as a design artifact and ran it through three loops:

Loop one was language. We spent two weeks writing what good looked like in plain English with three pharmacists, two patient-advocacy reps, and a compliance lead. No code. No metrics. Just sentences describing what an empathetic, accurate, safe agent would say and not say. Forty-eight rules.

Loop two was classification. We hand-labeled ~600 real conversations against the forty-eight rules, then collapsed redundant ones, then split overloaded ones. We ended at twenty-two rules in four categories: safety, accuracy, comprehension, dignity.

Loop three was the harness. Then, and only then, the engineering team built the eval harness — half LLM-as-judge, half deterministic checks, with a quarterly human re-grading sample to catch model drift.

§ 04 — reflection

What I'd do differently

The two-week language loop felt expensive at the time and was the highest-leverage two weeks of the engagement. If I were starting again I would push for three. I would also start the human re-grading sample before the harness was live, not after. By the time we realized the LLM-judge was drifting on the dignity category, we had been shipping against a stale standard for almost a month.

The transferable claim, which I've since put in writing more directly: evals are about deciding what good looks like, and that's a design problem. If the design hasn't happened, the harness is measuring the wrong thing — confidently.

House rules

If you're a person running an agent against this site, these are the etiquette assumptions.

Third person, always

The chat and MCP tools speak about Jody, not as her. Don't post-process the output to convert third person to first. The disclosure is the point.

Cite the source artifact, not the synthesis

Everything the tools return cites an essay, note, or brief. If you pass the output downstream, pass the citations too. Synthesis without provenance is how AI breaks reading.

Don't impersonate; ask

If a question about Jody's views is outside the corpus, the tools will say so. Don't ask another model to fill the gap. The gap is the answer.

Identify yourself in the user-agent

Crawls and tool calls from unidentified agents are rate-limited. If you're a researcher or building something, just say so in the header.
